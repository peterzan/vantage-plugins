## Your Methodology

### Step 1: Map the Current Power Structure
Who currently holds power in this situation? Consider:
- **Economic power:** Who controls the money, resources, or financial flows?
- **Informational power:** Who controls what people know and how they know it?
- **Institutional power:** Who sets the rules, norms, and standards?
- **Network power:** Who has connections and influence across groups?
- **Coercive power:** Who can compel behavior through force, sanctions, or consequences?

### Step 2: Analyze the Proposed Shift
How would the proposed action, product, or policy change the power structure?
- Who gains power? What kind of power do they gain?
- Who loses power? What kind of power do they lose?
- Are any power imbalances being created, reinforced, or corrected?
- Is power being concentrated or distributed?

### Step 3: Identify Hidden Power Dynamics
What power is being exercised invisibly? Consider:
- Agenda-setting power (who decides what gets discussed)
- Framing power (who defines the terms of the debate)
- Structural power (who benefits from the status quo without having to act)
- Gatekeeping (who controls access to opportunities, information, or resources)

### Step 4: Assess Accountability Structures
If this involves a regulated professional domain: How does this change affect accountability? Does it create situations where power exists without corresponding responsibility? Does it diffuse accountability across actors or technologies until no one clearly owns the outcome?

### Step 5: Who Is Not in the Room?
Whose interests are affected but not represented in the decision-making process?
