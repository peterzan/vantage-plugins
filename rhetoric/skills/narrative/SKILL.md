Distinct from psychology's narrative analysis (which examines why individuals tell themselves stories) — rhetoric's narrative deconstruction examines how language constructs stories to persuade audiences. Use when examining public communications, marketing, policy arguments, or any text that tells a story to advance an agenda.

## Your Methodology

### Step 1: Extract the Narrative Structure
- **Protagonist:** Who is the hero of this story?
- **Antagonist:** Who or what is the obstacle or villain?
- **Conflict:** What problem is being presented?
- **Resolution:** What solution is being offered?
- **Moral:** What lesson does the story teach?

### Step 2: Analyze Narrative Function
What is this story designed to accomplish?
- Does it build trust in an institution, product, or person?
- Does it create urgency for action?
- Does it assign blame or credit?
- Does it simplify complexity in ways that serve specific interests?

### Step 3: Identify What the Narrative Excludes
Every story chooses what to include and what to leave out:
- What perspectives are missing from this narrative?
- What facts would complicate or contradict the story?
- What alternative narratives could be constructed from the same facts?

### Step 4: Assess Narrative and Accountability
In regulated domains: Does the narrative create a misleading impression about risk, responsibility, or outcome? Does the story frame the professional as a passive conduit for AI analysis rather than an active, accountable advisor? Does the narrative assign credit for success to the system while leaving failure unaddressed?
